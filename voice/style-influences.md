# Style Influences

These writers and creators shape the voice we're aiming for. Take specific elements from each — never copy wholesale.

---

## Paul Graham

**What to take:** Intellectual honesty. The willingness to follow an idea to its logical conclusion, even when it gets uncomfortable. Starting with a genuine observation, not a pre-formed thesis. Short sentences mixed with longer ones. The confidence to say "I don't know" and mean it. The sense that the writer is thinking in real time, not performing.

**What NOT to take:** The essay length (PG writes 3,000-5,000 words; LinkedIn posts are 150-300). The tangents and footnotes. The Silicon Valley-centric worldview.

**PG patterns to adapt for LinkedIn:**
- "The way to figure out what to work on is by working." → This kind of recursive insight works brilliantly as a hook.
- "Boldly chase outlier ideas, even if other people aren't interested in them — in fact, especially if they aren't." → Contrarian framing without being contrarian for its own sake.
- Starting with a question everyone assumes has been answered, then showing it hasn't.
- The move from specific observation → general principle → surprising implication.

**Key PG principle:** "If you're interested, you're not astray." Write about what genuinely interests you, not what seems like it should interest your audience.

---

## Sriram Krishnan

**What to take:** The practical warmth. The "I've been in this meeting, here's what actually happens" energy. Structured but conversational — numbered points wrapped in narrative. Direct advice that respects the reader's intelligence. The balance between "I've done this" and "here's what you should do."

**What NOT to take:** Nothing major to avoid — Sriram's LinkedIn style is closest to our target voice.

**Sriram patterns to adapt:**
- "You've just gotten that job you've always wanted... But what do you actually do?" → The gap between expectation and reality is a reliable hook.
- "First, relax!" → Permission-giving moments that build trust.
- Mixing tactical advice (meet everyone in week 1) with strategic wisdom (pick an early win).
- The warm close that doesn't ask for engagement: "Trust yourself, have fun. And send me a note on how it goes!"

**Key Sriram principle:** Write as if you're giving advice to a friend who just called you, slightly nervous, asking for help. Not as if you're on stage.

## Harry Stebbings

**What to take:** The ability to distill someone else's expertise into a clear framework. The sense of curation — "I've interviewed 100 people, here's what matters." The topicality (reacting to news and trends quickly).

**What NOT to take:** The emojis, hashtags, and engagement bait formatting. The listicle structure that makes everything feel interchangeable. The breathless superlatives ("None has impressed me as much as..."). The influencer energy.

**Harry patterns to adapt:**
- Leading with a credibility statement that earns the right to teach: "I spent four years deploying AI to 50,000+ users" (Luca's version of Harry's "I've interviewed 100 growth leaders").
- The numbered insight format — but with more texture per point. Harry's points are 1-2 sentences. Ours should be 2-4.
- Reacting to recent news/events with a practitioner take, not just commentary.

**Key Harry principle:** People share frameworks. Give them something they can screenshot and reference later. But earn the framework through specificity — don't just assert it.

## Top VC Writing (Sequoia, a16z, GC, Thrive)

**What to take:** The conviction. VCs write with a point of view — they're not hedging. "We believe X" is their default mode. Also: the ability to spot patterns across many companies and synthesise them into a thesis. The long time horizons — thinking in decades, not quarters.

**What NOT to take:** The investor perspective (Luca is an operator, not an allocator). The self-congratulatory portfolio references. The tendency to describe markets rather than work in them.

**VC patterns to adapt:**
- The thesis-driven post: "We've seen this pattern across 50 enterprise deployments: the ones that succeed all share one thing in common."
- The "what most people get wrong" structure — stating the conventional wisdom, then subverting it with operator evidence.
- Naming categories and frameworks ("The Implementation Gap" is a good example — it feels like something a VC would coin).

**Key VC principle:** Have a point of view and defend it with evidence. Don't hedge.

## Jason Lemkin (SaaStr)

**What to take:** The obsession with specific metrics and benchmarks. "$10M ARR in 18 months" is more interesting than "rapid growth." The SaaS fluency that translates to B2B audiences. The practical founder advice that comes from pattern-matching across hundreds of companies.

**What NOT to take:** The repetitive format. The occasionally preachy tone. The narrow SaaS focus.

**Lemkin patterns to adapt:**
- Leading with a specific number: "€200M in trapped inventory. Found in 3 weeks. Freed in 12 months."
- The "most founders/operators/leaders get this wrong" opening — but with humility ("I got this wrong too").
- Defining clear stages or phases that readers can self-identify with.

**Key Lemkin principle:** Specificity is credibility. Vague insights get scrolled past. Precise ones get saved.

## The Synthesis: Luca's Target Voice

Take PG's intellectual depth. Sriram's practical warmth. The VCs' conviction. Lemkin's specificity. Harry's topicality.

Filter all of it through:
- An operator's perspective (not investor, not vendor, not consultant)
- European restraint (less breathless, more considered)
- Real deployment experience at massive scale
- Genuine curiosity about why things work or don't

The result: **the most credible voice in CRE × AI on LinkedIn** — because almost nobody in this niche has both the enterprise AI deployment depth AND the commercial real estate domain knowledge. That intersection is the moat.
